Comparison of the aging populations in China and Korea


1. Population structure

China and Korea have different population structures. China is the most populous country in the world, with a population of over 1.4 billion people. In contrast, Korea's population is much smaller, with around 51 million people. China has a more evenly distributed population across its provinces, with some of the most populated provinces including Henan, Sichuan, and Shandong. Korea's population is concentrated in urban areas, with the capital city of Seoul being the most populous.


2. Aging trend

Both China and Korea are experiencing an aging trend. As the population ages, the number of elderly people is increasing, and the fertility rates are decreasing. However, China's population is aging at a faster rate than Korea's due to the one-child policy that was implemented in 1979. The policy aimed to control population growth by restricting families to having only one child. Recently, the policy has been relaxed to allow two children per family



3. Elderly Care System

The elderly care system in China and Korea is different. China has a less developed elderly care system than Korea, with fewer nursing homes and less financial support for elderly care. In China, elderly care is often the responsibility of the family, and there is a lack of social security for the elderly. In contrast, Korea has a more established elderly care system, with a range of government-funded services and a higher number of nursing homes per capita. In Korea, the government provides financial support to families caring for elderly relatives.


(1) Zero pillar: Basic old-age pension plan

The Basic Old Age Pension Plan provides a monthly pension benefit of 100,000 won to 200,000 won for those who have reached the age of 65 or older, depending on their household income and meet the eligibility criteria. As of the end of 2016, the Korean government had paid a total of 10.3 trillion won in basic pensions, covering 4.58 million people, or 70% of the low-income elderly population.


(2) First Pillar: Public Pension Social Security System

The first pillar of the Korean pension system consists of the National Pension Scheme and the Special Occupational Pension. The National Pension Scheme is a contributory national sovereign pension fund, which is accumulative in nature, and the National Pension Operation Committee, established under the Ministry of Health and Welfare, is responsible for the direction of investment and the formulation of investment operation plans. The government sets and adjusts the replacement rate level according to the income and expenditure situation, and the national pension scheme members receive their benefits according to the replacement rate standard in the year they join the scheme. The Ministry of Health and Welfare is the primary regulator of the National Pension Plan, and it is also regulated by the National Assembly and the Office of the Ombudsman. The National Pension Plan covers all workers between the ages of 18 and 60, and the contribution rate is 9% of monthly income, with 4.5% contributed by the company and 4.5% by the individual, with monthly contributions from the employer. (The private sector and other residents contribute independently, and the Korean government provides some financial support to this group). The National Pension Plan pays a monthly old-age pension for life to those who have been in the plan for 10 years or more and are over 60 years old. (The age of entitlement will increase by one year every five years from 2013 to 65 in 2033).




Special occupational pensions include civil service, military, and private school teacher pension plans. The contribution rate for civil servants is 7% for the State and 7% for individuals until 2016, increasing to 16% in 2016 (8% each), and increasing by 0.25% for the State and 0.25% for individuals each year thereafter, to 9% for the State and 9% for individuals by 2020 (18% total contribution rate); for military personnel, 7% for the State and 7% for individuals; and for private school teachers, 8% for the State and 8% for individuals. Since the contribution rates and replacement rates of special occupational groups are higher than those of the general national pension participants (9%), this group of people will no longer participate in the second pillar pension.


(3) Second Pillar: Retirement Pension Plan

In 2005, Korea enacted the Employee Retirement Income Security Act, which established a retirement pension plan and initiated the second pillar of the Korean corporate pension system. The regulatory bodies are mainly the Ministry of Employment and Labor and the Financial Services Commission.

There are two types of corporate pensions in Korea: defined benefit (DB) and defined contribution (DC). The contribution rate for DB and DC plans is 8.3%, which is borne by the employer. The retirement pension plan is subject to the age of 55 and 10 years of membership.

In addition, employees participating in the DC pension plan can make voluntary catch-up contributions, which are withheld by the company, while the DB plan does not involve additional contributions due to the fixed benefits. The maximum amount of additional contributions per year for an individual is up to 18 million won for two or three pillars combined.


(4) Third pillar: individual pension plans

In order to meet the differentiated needs of the public for pension protection, Korea established a personal pension plan in 1994, and the main regulators are the Ministry of Planning and Finance and the Financial Commission. There are two types of individual pension products: pension accumulation products provided by banks, asset management companies, and insurance companies; and pension insurance products provided by insurance companies, with the total number of products exceeding 2,700. Participants of individual pension plans can receive monthly pensions from the age of 55 after making contributions for five years as agreed in the product contract.



4. Healthcare System

Both China and Korea have universal healthcare systems that cover their entire populations. However, Korea's healthcare system is generally considered to be more efficient and effective than China's. Korea's healthcare system is more decentralized, with a greater role for private healthcare providers. In contrast, China's healthcare system is more centralized and state-controlled. Healthcare in China is provided through a combination of public hospitals, community health centers, and private hospitals. However, the quality of care varies widely, with many rural areas lacking access to adequate healthcare services.



Advanced experiences that Korea has in dealing with aging


1. Community-Based Elderly Care System

Korea has a highly developed community-based elderly care system, providing seniors with a range of services, such as home-based care, day care, and institutional care. These services are integrated and coordinated to ensure that seniors receive personalized care that meets their needs. The government provides financial support for these services, making them affordable for seniors and their families.

2. Smart Home Technology

Korea is a leader in smart home technology, and this technology is being used to support the health and well-being of seniors. Smart home technology enables seniors to live independently in their own homes by providing features such as remote monitoring and assistance, fall detection, and medication reminders. This technology can reduce the burden of care on family members and provide peace of mind for both seniors and their families.

3. Age-Friendly Cities

Korea has been actively promoting age-friendly cities, where the physical environment is designed to support the needs of seniors. Age-friendly cities have features such as walkable streets, accessible public transportation, and well-designed public spaces. These features enable seniors to participate in social activities, access healthcare services, and remain physically active, contributing to their overall health and well-being.

4. Innovative Healthcare Services

Korea's healthcare system is highly innovative, with a focus on preventive care and early intervention. The government has introduced initiatives such as telemedicine, mobile clinics, and health promotion programs to improve access to healthcare services for seniors, especially those living in remote areas. These services enable seniors to receive timely and appropriate healthcare services, improving their quality of life and reducing the burden on caregivers.

5. Senior Employment Programs

Korea has implemented programs to promote senior employment, recognizing the important role that seniors can play in the workforce. These programs include job training, career development, and job placement services for seniors. By promoting senior employment, Korea is enabling seniors to remain active and engaged in society, contributing to their social and economic well-being.
